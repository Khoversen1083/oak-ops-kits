# Voice rules

The voice the coach writes in when drafting next-touch emails inside the
standup. Builder to builder, plain, direct, lowercase-comfortable. If a
rule conflicts with what feels natural to the student, the rule wins,
then they edit afterward.

## Hard rules (never break)

1. No em dashes. Ever. Use commas.
2. No emojis. None.
3. No AI jargon. No "leverage", "synergy", "robust", "cutting-edge",
   "agentic", "AI-powered", "best-in-class", "transformative",
   "next-gen", "world-class", "game-changer".
4. No filler openers. No "I hope this finds you well", "Just wanted to
   reach out", "Hope you are having a great week".
5. No filler middles. No "circling back", "touching base", "just
   following up", "per my last email".
6. Every follow-up references something specific from the original email
   thread. Not "checking in", a real callback to the prior message.
7. One ask per follow-up. Calendar link OR yes-or-no question, never
   both.
8. Never invent past clients. Never claim numbers the student cannot
   back up.

## 3 worked examples, bad vs good

### Example 1, FU1 (day 3)

Bad: "Just circling back on my email below to see if you had any
thoughts. Following up to keep this on your radar."

Good: "Quick follow on the dispatch rebuild thread, the part I am most
curious about is whether the morning route still routes through paper."

Why: the bad version is two filler phrases stacked. The good version
trades the ask for a yes-or-no the prospect can answer in 30 seconds.

### Example 2, FU2 (day 8)

Bad: "Wanted to bump this back to the top of your inbox in case it got
buried."

Good: "If the morning route is not your top headache right now, what is
the workflow your team complains about most?"

Why: the bad version is filler. The good version asks them to redirect
you to the real pain. Often gets a reply when the cold email did not.

### Example 3, FU3 (breakup, day 14)

Bad: "I will go ahead and close the loop here. Please don't hesitate to
reach out should anything change in the future. Wishing you all the
best."

Good: "Last note from me on this. If the dispatch rebuild becomes a
priority later, my email is the same."

Why: the bad version is corporate ghost. The good version is human and
done in 16 words.

## Soft preferences (calibrate to the student)

- Lowercase opener is fine if the original cold email used it.
- One contraction per sentence keeps the read natural.
- Match the student's own voice from their cold-email kit outputs if
  they ran it. If they did not, default to the rules above.

## When the student pushes back

If they say "this sounds too direct", show them the bad-vs-good table
above and ask which version they would reply to. Hold the line on the
hard rules, calibrate the soft preferences.
