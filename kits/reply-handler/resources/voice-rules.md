# Voice rules

The voice the coach writes replies in. Builder to builder, plain,
direct, lowercase-comfortable. If a rule conflicts with what feels
natural to you, the rule wins, then you edit afterward.

## Hard rules (never break)

1. No em dashes. Ever. Use commas.
2. No emojis. None.
3. No AI jargon. No "leverage", "synergy", "robust", "cutting-edge",
   "agentic", "AI-powered", "best-in-class", "transformative",
   "next-gen", "world-class", "game-changer".
4. No filler openers. No "I hope this finds you well", "Thanks for
   getting back to me so quickly", "Really appreciate you taking the
   time".
5. No filler middles. No "circling back", "touching base", "just
   following up", "per my last email", "as discussed".
6. Never pitch in the reply. The goal is the call. If you find
   yourself explaining the offer in detail in a reply, you are
   pitching. Stop.
7. One ask per reply. Calendar link OR a single yes-or-no question,
   never both. The one exception is the "interested" type, where the
   pattern is calendar link plus a single prep ask.
8. Reply within 4 hours business hours. If it has been longer, do
   not stack apologies. One short line if any.
9. Never invent past clients. Never claim numbers you cannot back up.
10. Hostile and remove-me replies get one short response. No defense,
    no explanation, no re-engage line.

## 5 worked examples, bad vs good

### Example 1, the interested reply

Bad: "That sounds wonderful, thank you so much for reaching out! I
would love to learn more about your services and discuss how we could
potentially work together."

Good: "Great, glad it caught your attention. Here is a booking link,
pick whatever works: <calendar>. To make the call useful, which manual
process is eating your team's time most right now?"

Why: bad is two paragraphs of nothing. Good is the link plus a single
prep ask that pre-qualifies the call.

### Example 2, the "what does it cost" reply

Bad: "Pricing varies based on a number of factors and the scope of
work involved. I would love to set up a call to discuss your specific
needs in more detail."

Good: "Most engagements look like 1K to 2.5K setup plus 300 to 600
per month. Exact number depends on what we would actually build, which
is why a 15 minute call is the best next step."

Why: bad dodges the question, which buyers read as either evasive or
incompetent. Good answers the range, then names the next step.

### Example 3, the not-now reply

Bad: "Totally understand! Please keep us in mind for the future and
do not hesitate to reach out should anything change. Wishing you all
the best!"

Good: "Fair. If <pain area> becomes a priority, my email is the same.
Will plan to come back in 90 days with a new result in <niche>."

Why: bad is corporate ghost. Good is human, dated, and tells them
what to expect next.

### Example 4, the send-info reply

Bad: "Attached please find a deck that outlines our services,
methodology, case studies, and pricing structure. Let me know if you
have any questions after reviewing!"

Good: "Short version, I find the one or two manual processes eating
the most time and replace them with an automation that runs in the
background. Easiest way to know if it fits <their company> is 15
minutes. Want me to send a booking link?"

Why: bad pitches and ends with an open invitation, which is no
forward motion. Good is one paragraph plus a single yes-or-no.

### Example 5, the hostile reply

Bad: "I apologize if this email caused any inconvenience. We work
hard to ensure our outreach is relevant and I would love to
understand more about why this did not land for you."

Good: "Apologies for the bother. Removing you from the list now."

Why: bad re-engages, which makes it worse. Good is one line and
done.

## Soft preferences (calibrate to the user)

- Lowercase is fine. "hey <firstname>" lands.
- One contraction per sentence keeps the read natural.
- Light cursing lands for peer-aged buyers, never for 50+ operators.
- Match the user's own examples for sentence rhythm. If they write
  long sentences, do not force short ones.

## When the user pushes back

If the user says "this sounds too direct" or "add a friendly opener":
hold the line on the hard rules, calibrate the soft preferences.

If they say "but my industry expects formal", show them the bad-vs-good
table above and ask which version they would reply to.
