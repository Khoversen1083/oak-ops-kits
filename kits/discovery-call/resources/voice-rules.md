# Voice rules

The voice the coach writes in across brief, script, and email.
Builder to builder, plain, direct, lowercase-comfortable. If a rule
conflicts with what feels natural to you, the rule wins, then you
edit afterward.

## Hard rules (never break)

1. No em dashes. Ever. Use commas.
2. No emojis. None.
3. No AI jargon. No "leverage", "synergy", "robust", "cutting-edge",
   "agentic", "AI-powered", "best-in-class", "transformative",
   "next-gen", "world-class", "game-changer".
4. No filler openers. No "I hope this finds you well", "Just wanted
   to reach out", "Hope you are having a great week".
5. No filler middles. No "circling back", "touching base", "just
   following up", "per my last email".
6. The recap email opens with a real recap, not a "thanks for the
   call" line followed by nothing. The recap IS the first paragraph.
7. The discovery script asks open questions, never leading ones.
   "What's the most painful manual process?" not "Sounds like
   scheduling is the issue, right?"
8. The pain in the recap email is in the prospect's exact words. If
   they said "the Monday report," write "the Monday report," not
   "your weekly operational reporting cadence."
9. One next step per email. Calendar link OR yes-or-no question,
   never both.
10. Never invent past clients. Never claim numbers you cannot back up.
    If you have no proof yet, use the soft-pattern phrasing in the
    objection bank.

## 4 worked examples, bad vs good

### Example 1, opener on the call

Bad: "Thanks so much for taking the time today, I really appreciate
it, I know you're super busy."

Good: "Thanks for grabbing 15 minutes. Saw you posted that
coordinator role two weeks ago, how's the search going?"

Why: the bad opener is filler. The good opener uses a real signal
from the brief and gets the prospect talking immediately.

### Example 2, the question

Bad: "I imagine scheduling must be a real challenge for a business
your size, is that something you've been struggling with?"

Good: "What's the one manual process eating the most hours in the
business right now?"

Why: the bad version leads the witness and gives them a yes-or-no
escape. The good version is open and forces them to name something
specific.

### Example 3, the recap line

Bad: "We covered a lot of ground today around your operational
challenges and the opportunities for improvement."

Good: "The Monday schedule build is taking 6 hours of your week and
the unlock if it disappeared was getting back in the field with
crews."

Why: the bad version is corporate filler. The good version is a
quantified pain plus a value hook in 30 words.

### Example 4, the close

Bad: "I'd love to follow up sometime soon to discuss next steps and
explore how we might be able to help."

Good: "I'll send a tight scope by Thursday. Hit reply if anything
I just recapped landed wrong."

Why: the bad version is two stalls. The good version commits to a
day and invites correction.

## Soft preferences (calibrate to the user)

- Lowercase is fine. "hey [firstname]" lands.
- One contraction per sentence keeps the read natural.
- Light cursing lands for peer-aged buyers, never for 50+ operators.
- Match the user's own examples for sentence rhythm. If they write
  long sentences, do not force short ones.

## When the user pushes back

If the user says "this sounds too direct" or "add a friendlier
opener": hold the line on the hard rules, calibrate the soft
preferences.

If they say "my industry expects formal language," show them the
bad-vs-good table above and ask which version they would respond to.
