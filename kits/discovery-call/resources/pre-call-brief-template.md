# Pre-call brief template

A one-page brief you read 5 minutes before the call. Not a research
dump, a decision-ready summary. Six layers. If a layer is thin, write
"thin, ask on the call." Do not pad.

## The six layers

### 1. Company

One paragraph. What they sell, who they sell to, rough revenue or
headcount range, location. Pull from their website's about page and
LinkedIn company page. If they have a recent press mention or
podcast, note the link.

### 2. Role

The person on the call. Name, title, how long they have been there,
what they owned at their last role. Are they the owner-operator, a
hired exec, or a delegated lieutenant? If they are not the day-to-day
decision-maker for the workflow you are scoping, flag it. A second
call with the operator is the next step, not a proposal.

### 3. Signals

What is going on in the business right now that suggests they are
ready to spend money on automation. Examples:

- Hiring posts in the last 60 days (especially ops, admin, or
  coordinator roles)
- New location, new market, new product launch
- A founder podcast or post where they named the pain themselves
- Funding round, M&A activity
- Team-page growth from quarter to quarter

If you cannot find a real signal, say so. Walk into the call with one
question to surface a signal instead.

### 4. Pains

What you suspect is eating their time, based on signals + niche
pattern. Be specific. Not "operations are a mess," but "the front
desk is rebuilding the schedule by hand every Monday morning." If you
have no hypothesis yet, list 2 to 3 common pains for this niche and
plan to ask which one is biggest.

### 5. Triggers

What forces a yes inside their company. Examples:

- A board meeting in 30 days where they have to report on a number
- An open req they cannot fill, that automation could replace
- Q4 budget closing, use it or lose it
- A churn or revenue miss they hinted at on a podcast

Triggers are the "why now." No trigger, the deal will slip a quarter.

### 6. Hooks

What you will reference in the first 2 minutes of the call to prove
you actually looked. One specific observation, not a compliment. The
hook is the open. Examples:

- "Saw your team posted a coordinator role two weeks ago, that
  usually means scheduling is breaking."
- "Listened to your interview on the X podcast, you mentioned the
  Monday report eats your week."
- "Noticed you opened the Austin location in March, second-location
  ops are usually where things crack."

## Layout (the actual file)

```
# Pre-call brief, [prospect company], [date]

Call with: [name, title]
Booked via: [cold email, referral, inbound]
Date / time: [day, time, tz]

## 1. Company
[one paragraph]

## 2. Role
[two to four sentences]

## 3. Signals
- [signal 1]
- [signal 2]
- [signal 3]

## 4. Pains (hypothesis, confirm on call)
- [pain 1]
- [pain 2]

## 5. Triggers
- [trigger 1]
- [trigger 2]

## 6. Hooks (use in the open)
1. Rapport hook: [one specific observation]
2. Backup opening question if call goes quiet: [one question]
```

## What NOT to put in the brief

- Your offer, your pricing, your case studies. The brief is about
  THEM, not you.
- Anything you cannot back up with a source. If you guessed, say
  "guess, confirm on call."
- A 4-page research dump. One page, scannable, decision-ready.
- AI-generated company history. They know their own history, you do
  not need to recite it.
