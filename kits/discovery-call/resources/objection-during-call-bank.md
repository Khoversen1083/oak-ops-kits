# Mid-call objection bank

6 objections that land mid-discovery. They look like resistance, they
are usually a chance to dig deeper. Each one has a one-line response
that keeps you in discovery mode, not pitch mode.

Keep this open on a second window during the call.

## 1. "What does this cost?"

The prospect wants the price before you have scoped anything. Do not
quote. Bracket and move on.

```
Honest answer, it depends on what we'd actually build, and we're
still figuring that out. Most engagements look like 1K to 2.5K
setup plus a small monthly to keep it running. Once I see the
workflow, I can put a real number in the scope. Cool if I keep
digging?
```

Then back to the next question on the framework. Never lead with the
range, never quote a specific number on the call.

## 2. "We already tried automation, it didn't work"

The prospect is signaling skepticism, not a real no. Find out what
they tried.

```
Most people tell me that. Usually it's because the build was generic
and not actually wired into how the business runs. Walk me through
what you tried, what broke, where it stopped working?
```

Their answer tells you what to scope around AND what to avoid. Worth
3 to 4 minutes of the call.

## 3. "Can you do [unrelated thing]?"

They are testing your scope. Stay in your lane, do not promise
everything.

```
Honest answer, that's not my lane. I stay tight to [your pain area
from intake] and the workflows around it. If [unrelated thing] is
the bigger priority, I'd say so. If [pain area] is still on the
list, happy to keep digging on that.
```

Saying no to scope you cannot deliver builds more trust than a yes
you will regret.

## 4. "I need to think about it"

Usually a deferred objection. Get them to name the real one.

```
Totally fair. What specifically do you need to think about? Want to
make sure I'm not leaving the wrong impression on something.
```

Whatever they name, that is the actual objection. Handle that one.
Do not accept "think about it" as the final answer on a 30-minute
call.

## 5. "Send me some case studies first"

They want proof before they invest more time. Do not send a deck.
Offer the next conversation instead.

```
Happy to walk through a recent build live on a 15-minute follow-up,
including what it cost, what we measured, and what would translate
to [their company]. That's more useful than a deck. Want me to send
a calendar link with the scope?
```

The scope email IS the next thing they receive. The follow-up call
is the call after that.

## 6. "Let me loop in [partner / GM / accountant]"

They are not the decision-maker, or the decision needs a second
voice. This is signal, not resistance. Adjust the close.

```
Makes sense. A few options: I can put the scope together and send
it to you to forward, I can share it with both of you, or we can
do a 15-minute follow-up with [partner / GM] before I scope it.
Whatever works on your end.
```

Pick the option THEY pick. Do not push for the second call if they
want to forward it themselves.

## When in doubt, do not pitch

If the objection throws you and you do not have a clean response,
default to a question. "Tell me more about that" buys you 30 seconds
and usually gives you the real objection underneath the surface one.

Never close to a price on the call. Never close to a "let me send you
some materials." The next thing they receive is the recap email,
inside 30 minutes, and the written scope, inside 48 hours.
