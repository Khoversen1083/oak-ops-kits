# The 6-question discovery framework

The canonical 6 questions Kevin runs on every discovery call. They are
in order. Each one earns the right to ask the next. Do not skip ahead,
do not rearrange. The order is the whole point.

The student can swap in their own custom questions in intake. The
defaults below are the proven set.

## The 6 questions

### 1. Why now?

"Why'd you decide to book this call? You didn't have to, what
compelled you?"

Why it's here: the answer reveals the actual trigger. If they say "I
saw your email and figured I'd check it out," there is no urgency,
the deal is months out. If they say "we hired two people last quarter
and our admin is drowning," there is a real trigger and a real
timeline.

### 2. What does the business actually look like right now?

"What does the business look like right now? Revenue range, headcount,
what's growing, what's not?"

Why it's here: grounds the call in reality. You cannot scope an
automation for a business you do not understand. Listen for size,
growth direction, where the strain is.

### 3. What's actually broken?

"If you had to name the one manual process that is eating the most
hours in the business right now, what would it be? Walk me through it
end to end."

Why it's here: this is the meat. Their answer is the pain you will
quote back in the recap and scope around in the proposal. Get the
specific workflow, the people involved, the systems, where the output
goes. Pricing-clarity moment lives here, get the hours-per-week
number out loud.

### 4. What have you tried?

"Have you tried anything to get there before? Another vendor, an
internal hire, software?"

Why it's here: tells you who your competition is and what budget they
have already approved. If they hired and fired an agency last year
for 8K, you know the ceiling. If they tried Zapier and it broke, you
know the level. If they have done nothing, the pain is recent or the
budget is new.

### 5. What changes if this disappears?

"If this thing disappeared tomorrow, what would actually change for
you and the business?"

Why it's here: surfaces the value beyond labor hours. The hours-saved
number is the floor. The "what would change" answer is the ceiling.
"I'd actually have time to chase the commercial accounts I keep
losing" is worth 10x the hours.

### 6. Who else weighs in?

"Who else needs to be in the room to say yes on something like this?"

Why it's here: identifies the real buying committee before you waste
two weeks scoping for the wrong person. If they say "just me," you
can move. If they say "my partner, the GM, and our accountant," you
need a second call or a buyer-ready proposal, not a quick scope.

## How to ask them

- Ask one at a time. Wait for the full answer before the next.
- Follow up on whatever they say is painful, do not rush to the next
  question.
- Take visible notes. Reference what they said earlier in later
  questions, proves you listened.
- If they get vague, ask one clarifying question. "Most of Mondays,
  is that 4 hours? 6? 8?"
- Do not lead the witness. "Sounds like scheduling is the issue,
  right?" is a leading question. "What's the most painful manual
  process?" is open.

## What the answers give you

| Question | Output |
|---|---|
| 1. Why now? | The trigger and the timeline |
| 2. Business shape | Scope context, ceiling and floor |
| 3. What's broken? | The pain in their words, the hours number |
| 4. What have you tried? | Competition, budget ceiling, sophistication |
| 5. What changes? | The value beyond labor, the recap hook |
| 6. Who weighs in? | The real decision path, second-call need |

By the end you have a quantified pain, a value hook, a budget anchor,
and a clear next-step path. That is what the recap email is built on.
