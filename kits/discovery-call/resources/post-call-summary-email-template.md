# Post-call summary email template

Sent within 30 minutes of hang-up. The recap is fresh, the prospect is
still warm, and the specific next-step deadline inside it is what
keeps the deal moving. Three short paragraphs. Quote them back to
themselves.

## Main template

```
Subject: [prospect first name], recap from today and next step

Hey [firstname],

Thanks again for the time today. Quick recap so we're aligned:
[process name in their exact words] is taking roughly [X hours per
week / Y people], and the biggest unlock if it disappeared was
[their answer to question 5, in their words], not just the hours
back.

I'm going to put together a tight scope, what we'd actually build,
the outcome, the timeline, and what it costs, and send it to you by
[specific day, 48 hours out]. Short, two pages, not twenty.

If anything you said today landed different than I just summarized,
hit reply and tell me. Otherwise expect the scope [day].

[your first name]
[your business email]
[your calendar link]
```

## Variables to fill

- `[firstname]`: prospect's first name. Casual is fine.
- `[process name]`: the exact phrase they used. If they said "the
  Monday schedule," write "the Monday schedule," not "your weekly
  operational planning workflow."
- `[X hours per week / Y people]`: the specific number you confirmed
  on the call. If they could not give one, write "roughly half a
  day of [role]'s time" and flag in the internal note that you need
  to firm it up.
- `[their answer to question 5]`: quote them. "I'd actually be in
  the field with crews," not "improved owner utilization."
- `[specific day]`: a real day of the week 48 hours out. "Thursday,"
  not "this week," not "soon."

## Why each part matters

- Recap with their exact words and numbers: they hear that you
  listened.
- The "biggest unlock" line: anchors value above labor savings, sets
  up the scope you send next.
- Specific 48-hour deadline: a real commitment. "I'll send something
  soon" is a stall. "Thursday" is professional.
- "Hit reply if I got something wrong": invites correction now,
  before they cool off.

## Variant: not the decision-maker

Use when they mentioned a partner, GM, board, or accountant who
needs to weigh in.

```
Hey [firstname],

Thanks for the time today, short recap below and a question on next
step.

[Same recap paragraph as the main template]

You mentioned [partner / GM / role] would need to weigh in before
moving forward. A few options: I can put the scope together and send
it to you to forward, I can share it with both of you directly, or
we can do a 15-minute follow-up with [partner / GM] before I scope
it. Whatever's easiest on your end.

Either way, I'll have something to send by [day].

[your first name]
```

## Variant: no clear pain surfaced

It happens. Do not manufacture a project to send something.

```
Hey [firstname],

Thanks for the time today. Honest take: I'm not sure we hit on the
right process to automate yet. Most of what you described sounded
like it's already running well, which is good for the business, less
of a fit for what I do.

I'm going to think on it tonight and either come back with a real
proposal tomorrow, or with a "let's stay in touch when [specific
scenario] becomes a thing" email. I won't make up a project just to
send you something.

[your first name]
```

A good share of the time they reply with "now that you mention it,
there's actually this OTHER thing." That is the real project.

## Anti-patterns

- Do not quote a price in the email. Price lands in the written
  scope.
- Do not promise specific savings or outcomes.
- Do not send a calendar link for "another quick call." The next
  thing they get is the scope.
- Do not BCC anyone. Do not add anyone to the thread.
- Do not apologize for the email length, the call length, or
  anything else.
- Do not paste `https://kevhov.ai/oak-ops` into the email. That CTA
  is Kevin's, not yours.
