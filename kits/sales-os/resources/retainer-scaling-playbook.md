# Retainer Scaling Playbook

The 3-question audit you run per client, the 30 percent minimum raise rule, the under-300 cutoff, and the scripts for communicating raises without losing the client.

For setup-fee and retainer math foundations, see `../pricing-retainer/CLAUDE.md`. This file is the SCALING layer, not the pricing-from-scratch layer.

## The hard takes (the kit enforces these)

1. Retainers under 300 per month are not retainers. They are favors. Raise to 300 minimum or drop the client.
2. Any raise must be 30 percent or more. A 10 percent raise confuses the client, signals you do not value yourself, and barely moves your MRR.
3. Drop low-margin clients before adding any hire. Capacity created by firing bad clients is free capacity.

## The 3-question audit (per current client)

Ask the operator these three questions about each current client. Score yes or no.

### Q1, Is the retainer priced right?

Compute their effective hourly rate: (retainer per month) / (hours per month I spend on them). Is that rate above 100 per hour?

- Yes, priced right
- No, underpriced

### Q2, Is the scope clear in writing?

Do you have a one-pager that says exactly what is included in the retainer and exactly what is out of scope? Can the client read it and not ask follow-up questions?

- Yes, scope is documented
- No, scope is fuzzy, you bill every time

### Q3, Are they painful to serve?

Last 30 days, did this client cost you more emotional energy than the next 2 clients combined? Slack pings at 8pm, scope creep without a "is that extra?" check, attitude on calls?

- No, they are easy
- Yes, they are painful

## The decision matrix

| Q1 priced right | Q2 scope clear | Q3 painful | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| Yes | Yes | No | Hold, no change |
| Yes | Yes | Yes | Drop or have the hard conversation, painful + paid-right is the worst combo (they expect more) |
| Yes | No | No | Document scope first, then hold |
| No | Yes | No | Raise 30 percent at next renewal |
| No | Yes | Yes | Raise 50 percent at next renewal, if they say no, drop |
| No | No | No | Document scope, then raise 30 percent |
| No | No | Yes | Drop |

## The raise script

When you decide to raise, the conversation is one paragraph, sent over email or said on a quarterly check-in call. Do not over-explain.

Template:

> "Quick heads-up. Starting [date, at least 30 days out], my retainer is going from [old number] to [new number]. The scope stays the same, the monthly call stays the same, the response time stays the same. Reason: I have built a more reliable monitoring layer and the value of what I am keeping live for you has grown. If you want to walk through it, grab time on my calendar. If not, the new number kicks in on [date] and I will adjust the invoice."

Do not apologize. Do not bury the number. Do not offer to negotiate.

## Why 30 percent is the minimum

- 10 percent raise on a 400 retainer: 40 extra. Not worth the awkward conversation.
- 30 percent raise on a 400 retainer: 120 extra. That is 1,440 a year per client. Across 5 clients, 7,200 a year. Real money.
- 50 percent raise on a 400 retainer: 200 extra. That is your "I want them to either pay or leave" raise.

Smaller raises also signal "I am not sure I am worth this" to the client. A confident 30 percent raise lands cleaner than an apologetic 10 percent.

## When the client says no to the raise

They asked you for a reduction or refused the new number. You have 3 options. Pick one and commit.

1. Hold the raise, let them churn. Best when they were already painful.
2. Trade scope for the old price (remove one deliverable). Best when they are pleasant but cost-sensitive.
3. Park the raise, revisit in 90 days. Best when they are about to give you a referral or case study.

Do not negotiate the raise itself down. If you raised to 520 and they offer 480, you said 520. Hold or trade scope. Never split the difference, that signals the number was made up.

## The "under 300" cleanup

If you have any retainer under 300 per month right now, this is the priority.

- Raise to 300 minimum, no exceptions, even if they have been with you 2 years
- If they say no to 300, drop them this quarter
- The "loyalty" rationale for keeping a sub-300 retainer is a sunk-cost trap, you are not loyal to them, you are loyal to the original deal you made when you were new

Run this cleanup BEFORE you add any new client at the new price tier. Otherwise your portfolio is permanently dragged down by the legacy rate.
