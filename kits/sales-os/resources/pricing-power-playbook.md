# Pricing Power Playbook

When to raise prices, how to communicate the raise, and how to kill low-margin clients without guilt. Reference for Steps 3 and 5.

For the per-deal pricing math, see `../pricing-retainer/resources/pricing-decision-tree.md`. This file is the SCALING pricing logic, not the per-deal logic.

## The hard takes (the kit enforces these)

1. Any raise is 30 percent or more. Smaller raises are not worth the awkward conversation.
2. Drop low-margin clients before adding a hire. Capacity created by firing is free.
3. You do not raise prices to "match the market." You raise because you are worth more, or you stop being worth your time.

## When to raise (the 4 triggers, any 1 is enough)

1. Your effective hourly rate on a client is below 100 per hour (retainer / hours per month)
2. You have not raised prices in 12+ months and your skill has compounded
3. You are getting referrals in your niche, the niche knows you, the niche pays
4. You are at capacity and adding any new client requires either firing or hiring

## When NOT to raise

- You are in the first 6 months of a client relationship and they have not yet seen 2 wins
- You raised in the last 90 days
- The client is the source of a referral that just closed (give them the referral discount for 6 months, then raise)
- You are about to deliver a major change to scope (raise after the change lands, not before)

## The 30 percent rule, again

A 10 percent raise on a 400 retainer is 40 extra per month. Annualized, 480. Not worth the conversation.

A 30 percent raise on a 400 retainer is 120 extra per month. Annualized, 1,440 per client, 7,200 across 5 clients. Real money.

A 50 percent raise is the "I want them to either pay more or leave" raise.

If you cannot defend a 30 percent raise to yourself, you are not ready to raise.

## The raise communication (one paragraph)

Send via email, or say on a quarterly check-in call. Do not over-explain.

Template:

> "Quick heads-up. Starting [date, at least 30 days out], my retainer is going from [old number] to [new number]. The scope stays the same, the monthly call stays the same, the response time stays the same. Reason: I have built more reliable monitoring and the value of what I am keeping live for you has grown. If you want to walk through it, grab time on my calendar. Otherwise the new number kicks in on [date]."

Do not apologize. Do not bury the number. Do not offer to negotiate up-front.

## Killing low-margin clients

Some clients are not worth what they pay, even at the right price. Signals:

- Below 300 per month retainer (always)
- They consume more than 50 percent above baseline hours
- They Slack at 8pm and expect a response
- They scope-creep without acknowledging it
- They are the reason you dread Mondays

When you decide to drop, the conversation is short. Email is fine.

Template:

> "Update. I am restructuring my client load to focus on [specific niche / specific type of work]. That means I am wrapping our engagement at the end of [month, at least 30 days out]. The automation will keep running, I will hand off the docs and a 30-day support window so you can find a replacement or take it in-house. Happy to recommend someone if helpful."

Do not justify, do not over-explain, do not negotiate. The decision is made.

## Why "fire before you hire" matters

Every painful low-margin client costs you 1.5x the hours of a good client at half the revenue. Firing one frees 3-5 hours per week and removes a recurring stress cost. That capacity is free. A hire costs 600-1000 per month and 5+ hours per week of management.

Always fire before you hire. The kit refuses to authorize a hire in Step 6 if there is an obvious firing candidate still on the books.

## What to do with the cash from a raise

The raise increases MRR. Three honest places that money should go, in priority:

1. Build operating cash up to 6 months of fixed costs (safety target above the 3-month floor)
2. Invest in the delivery template (Step 4 of this kit), so the next build is faster
3. Owner draw, only after items 1 and 2 are funded

Do not raise prices and then immediately spend the raise on new tools or a contractor. The raise compounds when it stays in the business.

## What to do when a client pushes back on the raise

3 options, pick one and commit:

1. Hold the raise, let them churn. Best when they were already painful.
2. Trade scope for the old price (remove one deliverable). Best when they are pleasant but cost-sensitive.
3. Park the raise, revisit in 90 days. Best when they are about to give you a case study or referral.

Do not split the difference. If you raised to 520 and they offer 480, you said 520. Hold or trade scope. Splitting signals the number was made up.
