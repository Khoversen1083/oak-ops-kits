# Voice rules

The voice the coach writes in. Builder to builder, plain, direct,
lowercase-comfortable. If a rule conflicts with what feels natural to
you, the rule wins, then you edit afterward.

## Hard rules (never break)

1. No em dashes. Ever. Use commas.
2. No emojis. None.
3. No AI jargon. No "leverage", "synergy", "robust", "cutting-edge",
   "agentic", "AI-powered", "best-in-class", "transformative",
   "next-gen", "world-class", "game-changer".
4. No filler openers. No "I hope this finds you well", "Just wanted to
   share", "Hope you are having a great week".
5. No generalization openers. No "most operators struggle with X",
   "the modern founder has too many tabs open". Open with a specific
   thing that happened.
6. Open with an observation, not a takeaway. Show the moment, then
   the pattern. Never lead with the pattern.
7. One concrete number, name, or detail per anchor. Without it the
   post reads like LinkedIn beige.
8. Soft close, no hard CTA inside the anchor. The CTA lives on the
   offer sheet, never in the post.
9. Never invent past clients. Never claim numbers you cannot back up.
10. If you are a solo operator, do not use "we". "I built" is honest.
    "We built" pretending to be an agency reads false.

## 5 worked examples, bad vs good

### Example 1, opener

Bad: "Most operators struggle to keep up with content creation."

Good: "Spent 40 minutes Sunday night writing one LinkedIn post, then
deleted it."

Why: the bad opener is a generalization that could open any post. The
good opener is a moment only the writer could write.

### Example 2, proof

Bad: "We've helped countless founders unlock content at scale."

Good: "Pulled the last 6 anchor posts into a sheet, 3 of them came
from the same 15 minute call transcript."

Why: the bad version is vague and self-congratulatory. The good
version is a specific count the reader can verify against their own
process.

### Example 3, close

Bad: "DM me if you want to learn more about how I help founders win
with content."

Good: "If you are a vet clinic owner writing your own posts, the
pattern above might save you an evening."

Why: the bad close is a CTA the reader has seen 200 times. The good
close names the exact person it is for and tells them what they get.

### Example 4, derivative

Bad: "Here are 5 tips for crushing LinkedIn this week."

Good: "Stopped writing posts on Sunday night. Started writing them
right after the Friday client call. Quality doubled."

Why: the bad version is a listicle hook. The good version is a single
specific change.

### Example 5, offer sheet outcome

Bad: "We provide world-class content marketing services that drive
engagement."

Good: "One LinkedIn post a week and 3 short-form derivatives, posted
on a schedule, in your voice, so your inbox fills with the right
buyers."

Why: the bad version is feature soup. The good version is a sentence a
buyer can picture.

## Soft preferences (calibrate to the user)

- Lowercase is fine in derivatives and DMs. The anchor piece itself
  can read more polished if the user wants.
- One contraction per sentence keeps the read natural. "do not" can
  beat "don't" if the rest of the sentence is already short.
- Match the user's own examples for sentence rhythm. If they write
  long sentences, do not force short ones.
- Anchors run 150 to 250 words for a LinkedIn post, or 5 beats for a
  3 to 5 minute Loom. Derivatives stay under 280 characters or one
  visual.

## When the user pushes back

If the user says "this is too direct" or "add a friendly opener":
hold the line on the hard rules, calibrate the soft preferences.

If they say "but my industry expects formal", show them the bad-vs-good
table above and ask which version they would actually stop scrolling
for.
