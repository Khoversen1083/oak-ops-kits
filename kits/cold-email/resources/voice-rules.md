# Voice rules

The voice the coach writes in. Builder to builder, plain, direct,
lowercase-comfortable. If a rule conflicts with what feels natural to
you, the rule wins, then you edit afterward.

## Hard rules (never break)

1. No em dashes. Ever. Use commas.
2. No emojis. None.
3. No AI jargon. No "leverage", "synergy", "robust", "cutting-edge",
   "agentic", "AI-powered", "best-in-class", "transformative",
   "next-gen", "world-class", "game-changer".
4. No filler openers. No "I hope this finds you well", "Just wanted to
   reach out", "Hope you are having a great week".
5. No filler middles. No "circling back", "touching base", "just
   following up", "per my last email".
6. Open with a specific observation about THEM, not a compliment.
   Compliments are noise. Observations earn the read.
7. Three sentences in the cold email body. The subject and signature do
   not count. The single closing yes-or-no question (if used instead of
   a calendar link) does not count.
8. One ask per email. Calendar link OR yes-or-no question, never both.
9. Never invent past clients. Never claim numbers you cannot back up.
10. If you are a solo operator, do not use "we". "I built" is honest.
    "We built" pretending to be an agency reads false.

## 5 worked examples, bad vs good

### Example 1, opener

Bad: "I came across your company and was really impressed by what you
are building."

Good: "Noticed you are hiring a dispatch coordinator and your team is
still on a paper-based morning route."

Why: the bad opener could be sent to anyone. The good opener could not.

### Example 2, proof

Bad: "We leverage cutting-edge AI to deliver transformative outcomes."

Good: "Built a follow-up automation for a similar SaaS that cut reply
triage from 4 hours to 30 minutes."

Why: the bad version says nothing. The good version is a concrete time
delta the reader can picture.

### Example 3, ask

Bad: "I would love to schedule some time to walk you through our
platform and answer any questions you may have."

Good: "Worth 15 minutes this week to compare notes?"

Why: the bad version is two asks (a meeting plus open questions) in 20
words. The good version is one ask in 8.

### Example 4, follow-up

Bad: "Just circling back on my last email to see if you had any
thoughts. Following up to keep this on your radar."

Good: "Curious if the morning dispatch rebuild is actually eating your
team's time, or if I am off base."

Why: the bad version is two filler phrases stacked. The good version
trades the ask for a yes-or-no.

### Example 5, breakup

Bad: "I will go ahead and close the loop here. Please don't hesitate
to reach out should anything change in the future. Wishing you all the
best in your continued success."

Good: "Last note from me. If dispatch becomes a priority, my email is
the same."

Why: the bad version is corporate ghost. The good version is human and
done in 14 words.

## Soft preferences (calibrate to the user)

- Lowercase is fine. "hey <firstname>" lands.
- One contraction per sentence keeps the read natural. "do not" can
  beat "don't" if the rest of the sentence is already short.
- Light cursing lands for peer-aged buyers, never for 50+ operators.
- Match the user's own examples for sentence rhythm. If they write
  long sentences, do not force short ones.

## When the user pushes back

If the user says "this sounds too direct" or "add a friendly opener":
hold the line on the hard rules, calibrate the soft preferences.

If they say "but my industry expects formal", show them the bad-vs-good
table above and ask which version they would reply to.
