# Proposal and Scope (read-only source)

The two-page document you send when the prospect says "send me something written." It is the pilot scope plus a cover note that frames the ROI in their language. PDF only, two pages max, numbers in bold. No tool names, no architecture diagrams, no "leveraging AI" boilerplate, no multiple pricing tiers, no legal language. The legal terms live in the separate retainer agreement, not here.

## The principle

After discovery, send a one-page summary, not a deck. Client outputs land in tools they already use, like email and a spreadsheet. They never log in to anything new. The labor savings is the floor, not the pitch. On the call the owner usually tells you what they would do with the hours back: close more deals, take on more work, stop missing things. That second-order gain is bigger than the hourly math. Put it in the proposal, anchored by the hard number.

## Cover note (top of the proposal)

```
[firstname], here is the proposal.

Quick context up top, then the scope, then the numbers. If anything looks
off, hit reply and tell me, easier to fix now than after we sign.

THE PROBLEM (in your words)
[2-3 sentences quoting them directly from discovery.]

THE BUSINESS CASE
- "[X hours] a week recovered means [$Y a year] of labor cost back."
- "Net of the retainer, you net [$Z a year]."
- "Setup pays back in [N months]."

WHAT YOU WOULD ACTUALLY GET
[3-5 bullets of concrete outcomes. What they receive, where, when.]

THE FULL SCOPE BELOW
[Then the pilot scope, sections 1-9.]
```

## Pilot scope (sections 1 to 9)

```
PILOT SCOPE, [Client name]
Prepared by [Your business]
Date: [date]

1. WHAT WE ARE BUILDING
   [One sentence in plain English. No tool names. No "AI."
   Example: "An automated weekly status report that pulls from your
   spreadsheet and lands in your inbox for review every Monday."]

2. THE PROBLEM IT SOLVES
   [2-3 sentences in their words from discovery.]

3. WHAT YOU WILL EXPERIENCE
   - [Concrete output 1: what they receive, where, when]
   - [Concrete output 2]
   - [Concrete output 3 if applicable]

   What you will NOT have to do:
   - [What goes away]
   - [What goes away]

4. THE NUMBERS
   - Hours/week saved: ~[X hours]
   - Annual labor cost recovered: ~$[Y]
   - Setup fee: $[Z]
   - Monthly retainer: $[W]/month
   - Net annual benefit: $[Y - W*12]

5. WHAT IS INCLUDED IN THE MONTHLY
   - Monitoring of the automation. If it breaks, I am the first to know.
   - Fixes when it breaks. No additional charge.
   - Adjustments when your process changes.
   - Monthly 30-minute check-in.

6. WHAT IS NOT INCLUDED
   Building a new, separate automation. If you want a second one, we scope
   and price it separately. The retainer covers what is in scope, fixed.

7. TIMELINE
   - Week 1: Gather access, build the automation, test on last month's data.
   - Week 2: 15-minute walk-through. You confirm. I tweak.
   - Week 3: Goes live. First real output lands [specific time].

8. PRICING
   - Setup fee: $[Z], invoiced upon signing.
   - Monthly retainer: $[W]/month, invoiced on the 1st starting [month].
   - 30-day cancellation, either side, no penalty.

9. NEXT STEP
   If this looks right, reply "let's go" and I will send the one-page
   agreement to sign. If anything is off, hit reply and tell me.

   [Your name]
   [Your business]
   [Email]
```

## Send-email body

```
Hey [firstname],

Proposal attached. Two pages, plain English. The numbers are based on what
you told me on the call.

Easiest way to move this forward is to read it, hit reply with "let's go" or
whatever you want changed, and we go from there. Happy to jump on a 10-min
call if it is easier.

[your first name]
```

## Follow-up cadence

```
Day 0:  Sent.
Day 3:  "Hey [firstname], wanted to make sure the proposal landed okay.
         Anything I can clarify?"
Day 7:  "Did this fit what you were expecting on price and scope, or do we
         need to adjust?"
Day 14: "I will plan to circle back in a couple months unless something
         changes, no pressure either way."
```

## Anti-patterns

- "Up to" pricing. Pick a number.
- Bullet lists of tech features. Section 3 is what they EXPERIENCE, not what you BUILD.
- More than 2 pages.
- Tool names.
- Corporate filler.
- Multiple pricing tiers or optional add-ons.
