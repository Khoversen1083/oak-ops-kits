# Objection Bank (read-only source)

The objections you hear most on a close call. For each one: the translation (what the prospect is really saying) and the line that handles it without overplaying. Internalize the translation, then say the response in your own voice. If you sound like a script, you lose the room. Fill the brackets with the deal's real setup fee, retainer, niche, and pain process. Smaller scope is always fine. Free is never an option.

Standing offer for filling brackets: setup [1,000-2,500] + retainer [300-600/mo].

## 1. "How is this different from [existing tool]?"
Translation: quick comparison, not committing yet.
"[existing tool] is great for [what it is good at]. The reason teams still spend hours on [pain process] even with it is that it does not connect to [the other system in their stack]. What I build is the connector. Usually 90 percent of the manual work disappears."
Do not trash competitors. Reframe yourself as the missing piece.

## 2. "What if it breaks?"
Translation: worried about being left holding a black box.
"Two things. First, I get the alert before you do. The system pings me, not you. Second, the retainer covers fixes. If it breaks, I fix it, no invoice. That is what the monthly fee is for."

## 3. "Can you guarantee it will work?"
Translation: they have been burned before.
"I will not guarantee revenue. I will guarantee the specific outputs in Section 3 of the proposal. If by Week 3 those outputs are not happening, I refund the setup fee. That is the bar."

## 4. "We do not have budget right now."
Translation: real no budget, or not painful enough yet.
"Fair. Two questions. Does [pain process] get worse next quarter or stay the same? And if you found another [retainer amount] somewhere, say by canceling something, is this the thing you would spend it on?"
The first answer tells you whether to follow up. The second tells you the ranking.

## 5. "Can we start smaller?"
Translation: they want a cheaper test.
"Yeah. We scope it down to just [smallest piece of the pain process]. Setup drops to [low end of range], retainer drops to [300/mo]. We add the rest in Phase 2 once you have seen it work."
Smaller is fine. Free is not.

## 6. "Our internal IT person can build this."
Translation: checking if they should DIY.
"Maybe. Two things. How loaded is your IT person, and have they worked with the systems this build touches? If they are slammed and this is not their domain, you are paying weeks of their salary to figure out what I would ship in two. If they have the time and the skillset, hand it over."
Do not fight on cost. Fight on speed and opportunity cost.

## 7. "Why is the retainer so high?"
Translation: they expected a one-time fee.
"It covers monitoring, fixes, and adjustments when your process changes. Without it, you would pay me hourly when something breaks. Worse for you, worse for me. The retainer means I keep it running. You do not manage me."

## 8. "Can we skip the retainer and just pay setup?"
Translation: they want to cut the recurring cost.
"I do not do that. The automation needs a person watching it. If it breaks at 2am and there is no retainer, neither of us has a clean answer for who fixes it. Either both, or I am not the right fit."
Say it calmly. Do not drop it.

## 9. "What if my process changes?"
Translation: worried about flexibility.
"Adjustments to the existing automation are part of the retainer. Change which column the data lives in, I update it. Change vendors and need a different system, that is a new automation and we scope it separately."

## 10. "Can I see what you have built before?"
Translation: they want proof.
If you have an approved live example, show it.
If you do not: "Most of my work is private. What I can do is walk you through a sanitized version of a similar build live. 15 minutes, I will share my screen. Want me to set that up?"
Never lie about proof. A sanitized live walkthrough beats a fake case study.

## 11. "We need to think about it."
Translation: unclear. Could be real, could be a polite no.
"Fair. What is the specific thing you are thinking about? If it is price, we talk scope. If it is whether [pain process] is the right thing to automate first, that is a different conversation. If it is something else, I would rather know now than chase a yes that is not there."

## 12. "Send me a more detailed proposal."
Translation: they want longer or fancier.
"I can. The two-pager has the outputs, the numbers, the timeline. What is missing for you? I would rather add the one thing you need than pad it with stuff you will scroll past."

## 13. "I would want to talk to my [partner / GM] first."
Translation: real, not a stall.
"Makes sense. Want me to put together a 90-second walkthrough you can forward, covering the proposal and the numbers? That way [partner] gets the same context you got, no extra call needed."

## When you hear 3 or more objections in one call
That is a soft no. They do not want to say it.
"Sounds like a few things are not sitting right. Honest read: is this bad timing, or the wrong project for you? If it is the wrong project, I would rather know now and stay in touch when something changes. I will not push."
Half the time they confess it is timing. The other half you save two weeks of follow-up.

## Common mistakes
- Do not recite the script. Internalize the translation, say the line in your own voice.
- Do not trash the competing tool. Reframe yourself as the connector they are missing.
- Do not drop to free or a free trial. Smaller scope, yes. Free, never.
- If you hear 3 or more objections, stop selling. Ask the soft-no question.
- Do not lie about past builds. A sanitized live walkthrough beats a fake case study.
